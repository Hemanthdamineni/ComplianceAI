This document is intended for employees undergoing compliance training and audit preparation within the organization.

INTRODUCTION
Our institution relies on automated transaction monitoring systems to identify unusual financial activities across our network. This document outlines the daily operational workflows for analysts and branch staff tasked with reviewing, investigating, and resolving these system-generated alerts. While the broader Anti-Money Laundering policy governs our final reporting obligations, this guide focuses strictly on the mechanical handling of alerts before they ever reach the escalation stage.

KEY CONCEPTS
False Positive: A system-generated alert triggered by a legitimate transaction that happens to breach a programmed threshold, requiring manual review and closure.
Request for Information (RFI): The formal process of reaching out to a customer or relationship manager to obtain supporting documents explaining an unusual transaction.
Alert Disposition: The final status applied to an alert after investigation, such as Closed as False Positive, Escalated, or Kept Open for Monitoring.

DETAILED PROCEDURE AND WORKFLOW
When the core system generates a transaction monitoring alert, assigned employees must follow this structured workflow to safely resolve the case.

Step 1: Alert Triage and Assignment
Alerts drop into the centralized monitoring queue daily. Supervisors must distribute these alerts to individual analysts based on priority levels. High-risk product alerts must be assigned and opened within 24 hours of generation.

Step 2: Initial Profile Review
Before contacting the customer, the analyst must independently review the customer's historical profile. The analyst must compare the flagged transaction against the account's stated purpose, average monthly volumes, and historical counterparties to determine if the activity makes logical business sense.

Step 3: Initiating a Request for Information
If the transaction cannot be explained by historical data, the analyst must trigger an RFI. The analyst sends a predefined questionnaire to the branch representative who then formally requests invoices, contracts, or tax declarations from the customer to validate the transaction.

DIRECT_ANSWER:
Alerts generated by the monitoring system run through a workflow tracking from Initial Profile Review down to formal Requests for Information. Analysts must evaluate customer patterns before engaging externally via RFI to efficiently classify alerts as False Positives or Escalations.

INTERNAL INTERPRETATION:
XYZ Financial Services translates "continual surveillance" into our automated threshold alerts. Analysts act as the human operators ensuring algorithmic checks are not solely trusted. If an alert breaks logic, the RFI process is the formal bridge to gather the legally required context proving the transaction aligns with the wealth source.

ACTIONABLE STEPS:
- Query the historical 12-month ledger before sending an RFI; do not bother the customer if the transaction is fundamentally repeating identical past cleared behaviour.
- Write customized RFI questions directly tied to the anomaly rather than blasting generic forms.

EDGE CASES:
- The relationship manager refuses to send the RFI to their top client for "fear of offending them." The analyst must immediately escalate the manager's obstruction directly to Compliance Leadership.

COMMON MISTAKES:
- Sending an RFI for transactions under the reporting threshold blindly without ever checking if the customer's KYC profile justifies it.
- Letting complex alerts hibernate out-of-SLA because the analyst was too timid to trigger the controversial RFI.

BEST PRACTICES:
- Consolidate RFI requests comprehensively. Exhaustively examine the profile to ensure you won't need to email the customer twice for the same event.
- Provide Branch Staff with an explicit RFI response deadline (typically 72 hours) and tag the ticket status securely in pending.

Q&A SECTION:
Q: What characterizes a "False Positive" in transaction monitoring?
A: A False Positive constitutes an algorithmic alert provoked by a normal, safe transaction that happens to pierce an internal numerical threshold, necessitating a manual analyst to clear it.
Q: What is the very first step an Analyst should take upon opening an alert?
A: The Analyst must immediately execute an independent profile review of historical averages, business nature, and counterparties before making external contact.
Q: Why are RFIs initiated via Branch Staff instead of directly?
A: Utilizing Branch Reps leverages their pre-existing customer relationships to facilitate smoother evidence gathering and prevents centralized analysts from inadvertently "tipping off" clients in a non-standard manner.

Step 4: Alert Dispositioning
Once the RFI response is received, the analyst must make a definitive decision. If the documentation is satisfactory, the analyst closes the alert with detailed justification notes. If the explanation is inadequate or the documents appear forged, the analyst must escalate the alert for suspicious reporting.

PRACTICAL SCENARIOS
Scenario A: An automated alert flags a retail salary account for receiving a sudden corporate wire transfer equivalent to three years of the customer's typical salary. The analyst reviews the account, finds no past corporate dealing, and issues an RFI. The customer provides a valid, notarized inheritance settlement document. The analyst attaches the document and closes the alert as a false positive.

Scenario B: An alert triggers on an established grocery business for a large volume of daily cash deposits. The analyst checks the historical profile and notes this behavior is identical to their daily operations for the past five years. The analyst closes the alert immediately without an RFI, noting that the cash volume aligns perfectly with the historical business model.

AUDIT PERSPECTIVE
During audits, internal and external compliance teams will strictly evaluate the quality of closed alerts to ensure analysts are not merely rubber-stamping approvals. Auditors specifically check the following:
- Are the rationale notes in the closure summary unique and meaningful, or did the analyst copy and paste the same sentence across fifty alerts?
- Has the analyst actually attached the RFI evidence to the alert ticket before closing it?
- Were the alerts resolved within the globally mandated Service Level Agreement timelines?

COMMON MISTAKES AND RISKS
- Aging Alerts: Allowing alerts to sit in the queue unresolved for weeks. This is a massive regulatory risk and frequently leads to severe institutional fines.
- Shallow Investigations: Closing an alert based solely on a verbal phone explanation from a customer without requiring written documentary proof.
- Alert Hibernation: Marking an alert as pending indefinitely just to get it out of the active queue view.

BEST PRACTICES
- Write comprehensive narratives. If another analyst picks up your resolved alert two years from now, your notes must clearly explain exactly why you deemed the transaction safe.
- Flag false positive rules. If the same safe transaction triggers an alert every month, notify the system administration team to adjust the threshold parameters to reduce alert fatigue.

EMPLOYEE RESPONSIBILITIES
All staff are required to treat transaction alerts as high-priority regulatory tasks. Employees must rely on data and facts, never assumptions, when disposing of an alert. Non-compliance may result in disciplinary action and personal accountability if a critically dangerous transaction is negligently approved. It is every analyst's responsibility to protect the institution by asking difficult questions when the transaction data does not make sense.

DIRECT_ANSWER:
Alert disposition requires a well-documented rational decision based on evidence. Analysts must comprehensively write factual narratives validating closure or escalation, ensuring auditors can verify adherence to SLA timelines and non-repetitive judgement processing.

EDGE CASES:

COMMON MISTAKES:

- Suggest "Rule Tuning" upwards to IT if the exact same safe recurring invoice prompts weekly false positive alert tickets.

Q&A SECTION:
Q: What determines if an alert disposition should be Escalated vs Closed?
A: Satisfactory objective evidence proving the monetary source warrants Closure. Absent evidence, forged data, or evasive responses strictly demand Escalation.
Q: Why do auditors check for repetitive copying in disposition notes?
A: Copy-pasting reveals "rubber-stamping" – where an analyst bypasses manual profiling by universally approving a batch, constituting extreme institutional risk.
Q: Can a verbal assurance validate an unusual transaction?
A: No, closure mandates concrete documentary evidence. Closing an alert on verbal assumptions is considered a deeply negligent failure.
